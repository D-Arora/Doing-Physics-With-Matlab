REFRACTION

 

           When any two- or three- dimensional wave traveling in one medium crosses a boundary into a medium where its velocity is different,

           the transmitted wave may move on in a different direction to the original the incident wave. This phenomenon is called refraction.

           One example as illustrated in the following animated gifs, is where water waves slow down upon entering shallower water and are refracted. 

 

           The speed of the wave v in a medium determines the medium’s refraction index n.

 

                        Incident wave (medium 1: refractive index n1)   speed v1     angle of incidence q1

 

                        Refracted wave (medium2: refractive index n2)  speed v2    angle of refraction q2

 

                       

 

           The direction in which the refracted ray travels is determined by Snell’s Law

 

                       

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The speed of a wave v is related to the frequency of vibration and the wavelength of the wave.

 

                       

 

           The frequencies of the incident wave f1 and the refracted waves f2 must be equal, hence the refracted

           wave’s wavelength changes upon entering the medium.

 

                       

 

           Hence, if the wave slows down upon entering the second medium, the wavelength of the refracted wave decreases.
